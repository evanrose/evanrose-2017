Another great thing about RSS (really and truly, I haven't been so excited about anything in a long time) is that I've been able to start reading sites I used to read but then stopped for whatever reason (like Anil's) and start reading ones I'd never have had time for in the past. Everyone else seems really excited about it too: Making news aggregators easy, Where Next for RSS?, and whole mess of feeds at Computerworld. I asked Brian what he thought about it and what he used, as well as why his entries show up as one or two line summaries, and this is what he said:

At the moment, I don't include entire articles in my RSS feed because doing so would bulk up the filesize quite a bit. Most browsers support Last Modified, Expires, and other HTTP headers used to facilitate caching (although people can usually change their browser's cache settings and fetch new copies of pages everytime, few people bother).

Until very recently few RSS aggregators were caching-aware (many still aren't). often, they grab fresh copies of feeds even if an HTTP HEAD request would have told the aggregator that the feed contents had not changed since the last time it'd fetched the file.

Add to this the fact that many people leave aggregators continuously running in the background, grabbing the feeds repeatedly at fixed intervals, and you can understand the effect that big feeds + dumb aggregators could have on a site owner's monthly transfer allotment.

I've experimented with aggregators from time to time but I haven't seen any significant advantage to an aggregator over a bookmarked group of tabs in Moz. One click and all of my daily visited sites pop up in my browser - plus I get to see stuff that can't/doesn't get distilled into an RSS feed.

I hadn't even thought about this. Thankfully, someone else has: HTTP Conditional GET for RSS Hackers.
